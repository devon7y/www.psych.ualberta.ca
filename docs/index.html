Results

We at the Computational Memory Lab study human verbal memory behaviour and its basis in cognitive and neural processes. There are several approaches we take in our research, including mathematical modelling, behavioural measures in the cognitive-psychology tradition, and measures of brain activity using electroencephalography (event-related potentials and oscillations) and functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). We like to address — and sometimes resurrect — classic studies and re-examine them in light of up-to-date knowledge and novel data-analysis techniques.

For example, it is common for people to have to keep two associations separate when they share a common item (e.g., first KIM–KRIS are paired, then KIM is paired with KANYE). In classic cognitive-psychology work that peaked in the 1960s–1980s, the prevailing view was that such associations are remembered independently of one another. By making specific changes to the data analyses — specifically, by including appropriate control measurements — the lab has shown, for the first time, that these kinds of associations do in fact compete directly with one another. The next goal is to pinpoint exactly what, both cognitively and in terms of brain activity, produces this competition and how people manage to overcome this challenge to memory — for example, bilinguals, who have two words for many objects.

At the heart of our lab's research are mathematical models of memory behaviour. This behavioural, modelling, and brain-activity work is designed to test model assumptions and address major ongoing debates. For example, some models assume participants learn an ordered list by linking each list item to another list item ("associative chaining"), while others assume participants assign each item a position or order value ("positional coding"). Recently, researchers have begun to investigate memory strategies that people voluntarily choose to use. Our procedures enable a higher level of experimental control in strategy research. Some strategies bear an uncanny resemblance to formal models — for example, the Method of Loci and the Peg-List method both resemble positional coding, whereas the Link method is a very literal analogue of an associative chain. Computational modellers and strategy researchers often work separately; current goals are to use instructed (compliance-verified) mnemonic strategies to inform debates between computational models, and to use ideas from those models to understand how those same strategies actually support memory.

The lab has a particular interest in oscillations — rhythmic brain activity — which are increasingly considered important for cognition, including memory. Dr. Jeremy B. Caplan co-devised, tested, and developed an improved means of quantifying oscillations (rhythmic brain activity), now known as Better Oscillation Detection (BOSC). The BOSC library is available in the supplementary materials of Whitten et al. (2011, NeuroImage), and the method is being adopted by other labs. BOSC is more selective for rhythmic activity than other approaches and produces more consistent results across recording sites, tasks, and even species.

The lab is part of the Alberta Cognitive Neuroscience Group and the Neuroscience and Mental Health Institute.
